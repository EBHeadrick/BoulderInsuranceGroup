Medicare

Medicare is a form of insurance available to people 65 and over, as well as people that are disabled. Boulder Insurance Group has specialized in helping qualified individuals secure medicare insurance for many years. Medicare consists of two parts: A and B. Everyone is entitled to part A, which covers hospital costs. Most people qualify for part B because they worked and paid taxes. There is a charge for part B, and it changes from year to year. Part B covers doctor office visits, and medical tests.

The basic costs of health care are covered at 80% by medicare, however Dental, Vision, and Hearing are not covered. Most people get supplemental insurance to cover the additional cost. If you qualify for Medicare but would like to further protect yourself with additional coverage, Boulder Insurance Group can help you choose the plans that best fulfill your needs.

In recent years, Part C (medicare advantage), and part D (prescription drugs) have been added.

Medicare Advantage Plans

The Medicare Advantage Plan for prescription drugs actually takes over as the primary insurance from medicare. This means medicare pays the plan (insurance company) to administer your health plans. Doctors and hospitals must have an agreement with your plan in order to get compensated.

Emergencies are always covered, but need authorization from the insurance company as soon as possible. The Medicare Advantage Plan also includes coverage for prescription drugs (part D). Many of these plans offer additional services like silver sneakers, and wellness counseling.

The Medicare Advantage Plan for HMO's:

The Medicare Advantage Plan for HMO's (Part C plans) take the place of Medicare Part A and Medicare Part B and may offer more benefits than Original Medicare. Medicare pays your Medicare Advantage Plan monthly to provide you with hospital and medical coverage. Original Medicare will pay for hospice care and some costs for clinical trials.

Medicare Advantage Plan Enrollment:

To enroll in Medicare Advantage Plan, you must be enrolled in both Parts A and B. You, Medicaid, or another third party must continue to pay your Part B premium. Another benefit of a Medicare Advantage Plan with Part D coverage is that you only need one Identification card for your hospital, medical, and pharmacy benefits.

Medicare Advantage Plans are designed to help you find the right plan at the right price for your own specific needs. They offer you protection from unexpected medical expenses, with premiums as low as $0.

A Medicare Advantage Plan may often use an HMO (Health Maintenance Organization) provider network. You will have to receive care from an in-network PCP (Primary Care Physician), or other in-network specialist, in order to receive benefits. You may choose your own PCP from a large selection of PCPs statewide.

Keeping the same PCP works best for your care because he or she gets to know you and your health needs when you go in for routine visits and checkups. Your PCP also maintains a record of your medical history.

When you need specialty care, you can count on your PCP to refer you to the right specialists. The concept of a PCP is central to all HMO plans; however, you can change your PCP at any time for any reason by simply calling the plan provider.

If you need emergency or urgent care services, you may go to the nearest health care professional that can help you. Emergency and urgent care services are covered at the in network rates no matter whether they were received from in-network or out-of network providers.

Your Medicare Advantage Plan will have a few enrollment periods you should know about.

The Initial Enrollment Period is for the 7 months surrounding your Medicare eligibility: This is the 3 months before you turn 65, the month when you turn 65, and the 3 months after you turn 65.

The Annual Election Period usually runs from November 15 to December 31st each year (these dates will change for 2011). This is the period you can enroll in or change your Medicare Advantage Plan. You may also switch to Original Medicare during the AEP.

New coverage begins on January 1st. The Annual Dis-enrollment Period is from January 1 to February 14. You may dis-enroll from your Medicare Advantage Plan.

During this time, you will be enrolled in Original Medicare and will have the option of choosing a stand-alone prescription drug plan. The Special Enrollment Period varies based on each person's situation.

Long Term Care

Long Term Care is Chronic Care. Chronic Care can be defined as Care given over a long time period without the expectation of the patient returning to good health.

Long Term Care can include services that are medical and non-medical in nature, and provide for the health and well being of the patient. Often, Long Term Care does not require skilled medical professionals; it begins at home with assistance provided by family members, neighbors, or friends. Loved ones acting as caregivers deliver Custodial Care, which includes transportation, shopping, managing finances, and help with the 6 activities of daily living. These activities of daily living are clearly defined in the medical, and insurance community; They are eating, bathing dressing, toileting, continence, and transferring (eg. Moving from bed to a chair). Sometimes a patient will require constant observation and assistance for dementia.

Long Term Care can be provided in a variety of settings:

  • Long_Term_CareHome Care
  • Nursing Home
  • Assisted Living Facilities
  • Skilled Nursing Facilities
  • Hospice Facilities
are the most common places to receive care. Because Long Term Care usually does not require the assistance of skilled medical professionals, it is usually not covered by health insurance or by Medicare.

Most Long Term Care is financed by private funds (self-insurance), Medicaid, or by Long Term Care Insurance. Term

Term Life Insurance costs less than whole life insurance, but does not build cash value. It lasts for a specific number of years, or term. Usually the term is 10, 20, or 30 years. The cost is based on age at issue, and stays the same thru out the term. Some term policies can be converted to whole life without evidence of insurability. This is an excellent way to start out with the coverage amount you need, and convert to whole life as your financial situation improves.

Whole

Whole Life Insurance is for your whole life. The cost is determined based on your age when the policy is issued, and it lasts until the end of your life. Some policies are paid up in one lump sum, some have premiums for 10 or 20 years, and some are paid up at age 65. Whole life policies build cash value that can be used later. Money can be surrendered, or taken out as a loan, but be careful! If money is not drawn out properly, it can be a taxable event, or even cause the policy to lapse prematurely.

Estate planning includes wills, directives, and trusts. The basic concept is to maximize the money that goes to your family or favorite charity, and to ensure that your final wishes are carried out. The following is for informational purposes, to support our valued customers: Estate Planning - Wills A will is a legal document that determines the succession of your estate. Living Trusts

Trusts are an arrangement where real or personal property is held by a trustee for the benefit of another person (the beneficiary). There are two basic kinds of trusts; testamentary trusts are formed at your death and a living trust is established during your lifetime. In a funded living trust, the settlor puts money and property into their trust during their lifetime for their own benefit, and possibly for the benefit of others. When a person is capable of managing their own affairs, they usually name themselves as trustee of the living trust they establish. The duties of the trustee are to invest and distribute the assets of the trust to the settlor and beneficiaries. Choosing a Revocable or Irrevocable Living Trust. Living trusts are almost always revocable, which means the settlor can revoke or amend the trust as long as they live, or as long as they are capable of managing their own affairs. Alternate trustees can be named to take over the trustee duties if the settlor becomes disabled or unable to perform their duties. When the settlor dies, the living trust usually terminates, and the successor trustee distributes the trust property to the beneficiaries, similar to the distribution from a will. Often times, a living trust will continue after the death of the settlor in order to provide benefits to the settlor’s spouse and family. The living trust will then terminate at a later date. If a living trust is set up properly, and fully funded (all of the settlor’s assets are put into the trust), there can be several advantages. A fully funded living trust can eliminate the need for probate upon the death of the settlor. If a settlor owns property in multiple states, it can possibly eliminate the need for probate in multiple states. A living trust may eliminate the need for conservatorship if the settlor becomes disabled or unable to fulfill the duties of trustee. However, a separate medical power of attorney is needed in order to make medical decisions for the settlor. The terms of a living trust are generally private whereas the terms of a will are public. Your Living Trust and Taxes: If a living trust is not drafted correctly, there can be significant harmful tax liabilities. It’s usually better to have professional assistance in drafting either a testamentary or living trust instead of trying to do it yourself, or by purchasing a preprinted trust form. It’s best for you to keep an up to date list of all your property and debts. Things to include are insurance policies, securities, bank accounts, real estate, artwork, jewelry, business interests, pension plans, IRA’s, and other retirement benefits. You should keep this list with your other important documents. It’s best to keep a list of your current financial advisors, your attorney, and your accountant in a safe, easy to find place also. There are many variables to weigh in the creation of a living trust so professional help is a must to be sure your wishes are honored.
